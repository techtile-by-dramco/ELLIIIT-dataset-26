\section{Platform and Acquisition Workflow}
\label{sec:platform}

\dataset{} is collected inside Techtile, a room-scale distributed testbed intended for communication, sensing, positioning, and related 6G experiments~\cite{techtilePrimer,techtileOpen6g}. Techtile is built around 140 detachable tiles with Ethernet-based power, communication, and synchronization, and later experiments on the platform have further demonstrated Qualisys-based sub-centimeter tracking, robotic 2D sampling, and phase-coherent RF operation over 41--42 ceiling elements at 920~MHz~\cite{syncWptTechtile,geomWpt2026,dliSuppression2026}. For the campaign documented in this paper, the measurement rig combines an ultrasonic speaker and an \gls{rf} \gls{ue} antenna on a common mechanical mount so that the acoustic and \gls{rf} channels are sampled at the same physical rover location. Ground-truth position is provided by the Qualisys system referenced in the experiment settings.

\subsection{Positioning and Measurement Procedure}

The rover follows a reproducible seven-sweep multi-resolution scan over an approximately $1.23 \times 1.23$~m work area. The motion planner alternates horizontal and vertical serpentine passes and progressively reduces the waypoint spacing from 120~mm to about 50~mm, so later sweeps densify regions already visited by the coarse passes. This provides dense spatial coverage while keeping the sampling pattern automated and repeatable.

Ground-truth positioning is provided by the Qualisys system configured to track the rigid body \texttt{ELLIIT}. After every completed rover move, the orchestrator requests one fresh position update, suppresses stale repeats, and appends \texttt{experiment\_id}, \texttt{cycle\_id}, \texttt{meas\_id}, UTC capture time, measured $(x,y,z)$ coordinates, and the reported rotation matrix to the per-experiment rover CSV. The position sample is therefore part of the acquisition cycle itself rather than an offline interpolation step.

The acquisition stack is organized around two control layers. The deployment layer pushes code and settings to the clients and starts the long-lived services. The orchestration layer then coordinates one end-to-end measurement cycle across rover motion, position logging, acoustics, and \gls{rf} synchronization. The measurement semantics are explicit in the code path:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the rover moves to the commanded waypoint;
    \item the system records one fresh Qualisys position sample at that stop;
    \item the acoustic client records one chirp-response capture;
    \item the \gls{rf} orchestrator triggers one synchronized \gls{rf} cycle across the participating tiles.
\end{enumerate}
The important consequence is that \texttt{(experiment\_id, cycle\_id)} is the fundamental alignment key throughout \dataset{}.

\subsection{RF Measurements}

The active \gls{rf} aperture used by the reciprocity workflow is the Techtile ceiling group: 42 receiver tiles (\texttt{A05} through \texttt{G10}), one synchronized pilot transmitter (\texttt{T01}), and one separate continuous reference transmitter (\texttt{REF01}). The campaign configuration sets the \gls{rf} measurements to 920~MHz with a 250~kS/s sample rate and receiver gain 80.

At each rover stop, the \gls{rf} client waits for an \texttt{ALIVE} quorum from the synchronized participants, inserts a short pre-sync delay, publishes a cycle-specific \texttt{SYNC} message, and waits for the corresponding \texttt{DONE} quorum before reporting completion to the main orchestrator. This cycle-level trigger is consistent with the broader Techtile synchronization chain of pulse-per-second distribution for time alignment, a shared 10~MHz reference for frequency alignment, and reciprocity-based calibration for front-end phase alignment~\cite{syncWptTechtile}. For phase-sensitive \gls{rf} positioning, that synchronization is part of the measurement definition rather than a background implementation detail.

Each receiver stores one pilot-response record per cycle containing at least the experiment and cycle identifiers together with the measured pilot phase and amplitude. The reciprocity client estimates a circular-mean pilot phase from the captured IQ samples and pairs it with an \gls{rms} pilot amplitude; post-processing then applies cable-phase correction and converts the result into one complex \gls{csi} value per hostname. A single physical stop therefore produces a vector of up to 42 complex \gls{csi} observations across the active ceiling receivers.

The acoustic modality uses the rover-mounted ultrasonic speaker as the active source and the distributed room microphones as fixed receivers. One acoustic capture emits a 30~ms logarithmic chirp from 20 to 40~kHz at a 250~kS/s sample rate. The acquisition task records a window twice as long as the excitation so that the direct arrival and the early room response are preserved in the raw trace; in the current release configuration, these received chirp traces are stored directly rather than being reduced to only deconvolved impulse responses.

On the hardware side, the acoustic script shares the PXIe\_Clk100 reference between output and input tasks and uses hardware start triggering so that the speaker emission and microphone sampling are aligned within one capture. Each saved acoustic CSV stores the chirp settings together with a microphone label, microphone coordinates, and the recorded waveform samples. The acoustic parser later reorganizes these runtime files into per-experiment waveform tensors indexed by experiment, cycle, microphone label, and sample index. Because the acoustic source and the \gls{rf} antenna are co-located on the rover, the acoustic traces and \gls{rf} snapshots refer to the same physical rover pose.

\Cref{tab:config} highlights why \dataset{} is useful beyond one narrow experiment script. The campaign is based on a reproducible measurement protocol, explicit synchronization settings, and a consistent motion pattern rather than ad-hoc manual sampling. In particular, the rover planner uses a seven-sweep multi-resolution scan whose spacing is progressively reduced from 120 mm to approximately 50 mm. This makes the measurement process dense enough for spatial visualization while remaining operationally tractable for a multi-modal campaign.

Post-processing begins after acquisition. The \gls{rf} parser scans the per-host result files, extracts complex pilot measurements, applies cable correction, joins the result with rover positions, filters consecutive duplicate positions using a tolerance derived from the rover configuration, and writes one merged NetCDF. The acoustic parser produces a separate per-experiment NetCDF of recorded waveforms. \dataset{} therefore preserves both the runtime logs and the processed analysis products.
